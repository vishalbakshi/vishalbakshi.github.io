administrative burdens: how data and reporting requirements further tips the scales against our most vulnerable schools

i suppose this is somewhere between an informative and persuasive essay. i will present some information as evidence for the three following observations:

  1. the people buying the software are usually not the ones managing the data
  2. managing a large amount of data and its business rules requires a high level of technical literacy
  3. the impact of data management on school funding is extremely high

who manages a school's data?

in a large district, a data team manages all schools' data, ideally with responsive communication channels to the front office staff at each school. at smaller districts, charter networks or individual charter schools, you're lucky to find a full time data manager on staff, and usually find registrars or other front office staff managing data along with other competng responsibilities. however, the decisions around software and data management are made at the leadership level, usually by people who are not in the trenches with the data. this creates a major disconnect: vendors cater their sales and user experience for not those who will use their products and services everyday, but those that will sign the contract.

how much data is there to manage?

what does the data impact?

the latest data i could find for california school funding was 2018-2019 financial data on Ed-Data.

54% of all school funding (about $40 Billion) depends on data submitted by schools. this means that incorrectly reporting a single student's data can cost the school about $7500. schools that have regularly changing enrollments (homelessness, foster youth, refugees, or new immigrants) often lack robust organizational practices to keep with data management needs.

26% of all school funding (about $19 Billion) comes from property taxes. schools that are located in low-income neighborhoods start with a lower budget regardless of how they manage their data.

where do we look for solutions?

it's important to start this section by acknowledging that some responsibility lies in the hands of the schools, districts and their board members. organizational disconnects between those who manage data and those who manage the budget can only be solved at the organizational level. prioritizing data management, at some level, is a choice.

it's equally important to follow that up by acknowledging that how property taxes are allocated, what the data requirements are designed to be, and what legislation those business rules are designed around all affect school funding.

lastly, the education tech industry has a huge role to play in data management. tangible features based on user experience can determine how easily a school can manage data. there are engineering choices that can be made to prioritize data management. it's not superficially sexy like analytics. it's fundamentally necessary for the existence of our public schools.

a discussion on the war between charter schools and the state is outside the scope of this piece, but it adds additional strains to the existing complexity of the system for everyone involved. fundamentally, i think that the privatization of essential needs such as education is dangerous to the health of a democratic public. i also know firsthand that there is good, necessary work being done by the people working at charter schools who genuinely care about the well-being of their students. however, i think that the flaws of non-charter public schools will not be solved by privatization, but by national public education reform. again, all of this is out of scope for this piece of writing and outside of my expertise, so i suggest you look elsewhere for a more productive discussion about education reform.
