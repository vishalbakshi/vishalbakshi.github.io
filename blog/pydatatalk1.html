administrative burdens: how data and reporting requirements further tips the scales against our most vulnerable schools

i suppose this is somewhere between an informative and persuasive essay. i will present some information as evidence for the four following conclusions:

  1. the people buying the software are usually not the ones managing the data
  2. managing a large amount of data and its business rules requires a high level of technical literacy
  3. the impact of data management on school funding is extremely high

who manages a school's data?

in a large district, a data team manages all schools' data, ideally with responsive communication channels to the front office staff at each school. at smaller districts, charter networks or individual charter schools, you're lucky to find a full time data manager on staff, and usually find registrars or other front office staff managing data along with other competng responsibilities. however, the decisions around software and data management are made at the leadership level, usually by people who are not in the trenches with the data. this creates a major disconnect: vendors cater their sales and user experience for not those who will use their products and services everyday, but those that will sign the contract.

how much data is there to manage?

throughout the school year, there are about 415 unique fields recorded for each student in a student information system. these fields are exported across 18 different extracts which are then uploaded to the state database via a web portal. next, various business rules (derived from the education code) are applied to this data which then comes out onto 82 official reports with a total of 1394 fields (many of which are aggregated counts grouped by different categories). most of the data products that i create present this data in a more human readable way to help schools analyze it.
